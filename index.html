First Aid
🟢 What TO DO During Active Seizure ▶
  • ✓
    Protect from injury — clear hard/sharp objects, cushion the head
  • ✓
    Time the seizure — critical; >5 min = status epilepticus threshold
  • ✓
    Recovery position (lateral decubitus) — ideally after convulsive phase or if already on side
  • ✓
    Loosen tight clothing around neck
  • ✓
    Stay with the patient until fully recovered
⚡
Move ONLY if danger: road, water, stairs, or near fire. Drag by armpits as a unit, minimal handling, then resume protective positioning.
🔴 What NOT TO DO ▶
  • ✗
    Do NOT restrain movements — injury risk from forced restraint
  • ✗
    Do NOT put anything in the mouth — tongue swallowing is a myth; airway injury is real
  • ✗
    Do NOT give water or food
  • ✗
    Do NOT leave them alone
  • ✗
    Do NOT aggressively reposition unless in immediate danger

First Aid
🚨 Scene Safety — Reach, Throw, Don't Go ▶
🔴
Do NOT enter the water unless you are a trained water rescuer. Panicking drowning victims can incapacitate rescuers — you risk becoming a second victim.
  • 1
    Reach — extend a pole, rope, clothing, or branch from the shore
  • 2
    Throw — flotation device, rope, anything buoyant
  • 3
    Don't go — last resort only, trained rescuers only

💓 CPR Algorithm — Ventilation First ▶
RESPONSIVE + breathing normally? → Lateral decubitus (recovery position) → Keep warm (remove wet clothing) → Monitor, activate EMS UNRESPONSIVE or abnormal breathing? → 5 RESCUE BREATHS FIRST ← drowning is hypoxic arrest → Check pulse → If no pulse: CPR 30:2 → AED as soon as available → Continue until EMS arrives
💡
Key difference from cardiac CPR: Drowning = hypoxic arrest. Ventilation is the priority. Start with 5 rescue breaths BEFORE chest compressions.
ER Management
🔍 Primary Survey — ABCDE ▶
A
Airway
Patent? Vomit, debris, laryngospasm? If GCS ≤8 or unprotected airway → intubate early, do not wait for deterioration.
B
Breathing
SpO₂, RR, work of breathing. Auscultate — crackles = pulmonary edema/aspiration. CXR immediately (may be normal initially). Target SpO₂ >94%, avoid hyperoxia.
C
Circulation
Hypothermia → bradycardia, arrhythmias, hypotension. 12-lead ECG (Osborn waves?). IV access ×2, warm IV fluids if hypothermic.
D
Disability
GCS, pupils, blood glucose.
E
Exposure
Core temperature (rectal or esophageal probe). Full skin exam for trauma. Remove all wet clothing.
🫁 Pulmonary Management ▶
  • →
    ARDS protocol if intubated — low tidal volume 6 ml/kg IBW, PEEP titration
  • →
    CPAP / HFNC as bridge — good option for moderate hypoxia before intubation
  • →
    Surfactant washout is the main pulmonary insult — treat like ARDS
  • →
    Antibiotics — NOT routinely indicated. Use only for contaminated water (sewage, pond) or signs of infection
  • →
    Serial CXR at 4–6 hours — pulmonary edema can evolve after initial normal film

GradeClinical PictureDisposition
1Cough only, normal lungsObserve 6–8h; discharge if stable
2Crackles at bases, SpO₂ okAdmit, monitor
3Pulmonary edema, low SpO₂, normotensiveICU
4Pulmonary edema + hypotensionICU

⚠️
No drowning victim should be discharged without ≥6–8 hours observation, even Grade 1. Secondary drowning (delayed pulmonary edema) is real and can be fatal.
💡 Key Pearls ▶
  • ◆
    "Not dead until warm and dead" — continue resuscitation even after prolonged cold-water submersion until core temp ≥32–35°C
  • ◆
    Cold water is neuroprotective — lower metabolic demand; better neurological outcomes possible even after extended submersion
  • ◆
    C-spine immobilization only if indicated — witnessed dive/trauma. Routine immobilization delays CPR unnecessarily
  • ◆
    Salt vs fresh water — minimal acute clinical difference; both cause surfactant washout and ARDS pattern
Classification
🌡️ Swiss System — Grading & Clinical Features ▶
HT I 32 – 35°C
Mild — Conscious, shivering
HT II 28 – 32°C
Moderate — Impaired consciousness, shivering stops
HT III 24 – 28°C
Severe — Unconscious, vital signs present
HT IV < 24°C
Arrest — No vital signs
HT V Irreversible
Death from hypothermia confirmed
💡
Key clinical sign: Shivering stops ~30–32°C. Its absence signals you've crossed into moderate territory — do not be reassured by a quiet patient.

🎯 Target Temperature & Rewarming Rate ▶
Target Temperature
36–37.5°C
Normothermia — avoid overshooting
Rewarming Rate
~1°C/hr
For moderate–severe hypothermia
GradeTempRewarming Strategy
HT I — Mild 32–35°C Passive — blankets, warm dry environment, remove wet clothing
HT II — Moderate 28–32°C Active external — warming blankets, heating pads to trunk, warm humidified O₂, warm IV fluids (38–42°C)
HT III — Severe 24–28°C Active internal — warm IV fluids, warm humidified O₂, body cavity lavage (gastric, bladder, pleural, peritoneal)
HT IV — Arrest <24°C ECMO / ECPR — extracorporeal rewarming if available; most effective in arrested patients
Post-Arrest
🧊 Targeted Temperature Management (TTM) — Post-ROSC ▶
ScenarioTargetDuration
Post-ROSC, comatose (cardiac arrest) 32–36°C 24 hours, then controlled rewarming
Actively hypothermic arrest Rewarm to normothermia first Reassess neuro after warm
Post-ROSC, avoid >37.5°C Hyperthermia is clearly harmful
📖
TTM2 Trial (2021): Shifted practice toward 36°C rather than 33°C for most post-arrest patients. Key principle is preventing hyperthermia rather than achieving a specific low target.
Pitfalls
⚠️ Critical Pitfalls & Clinical Traps ▶
PitfallDetail
Afterdrop Core temp continues falling even after rewarming starts — cold blood from periphery floods core. Don't panic; keep rewarming.
Rewarming arrhythmias VF risk highest during rewarming in HT III–IV. Handle gently, minimal stimulation, continuous cardiac monitoring.
Potassium >12 mEq/L Futility marker — suggests irreversible cell death. Consider stopping ECMO/CPR in hypothermic arrest.
Declaring death too early "Not dead until warm and dead" — do not declare until core temp ≥32–35°C has been achieved and attempted.
Osborn (J) waves Pathognomonic ECG finding — but not always present. More prominent at lower temps. Don't rule out hypothermia by ECG alone.
Peripheral warming too aggressive Causes vasodilation → distributive shock (rewarming shock). Warm the trunk first, not extremities.
Drug metabolism impaired Hypothermia slows drug clearance — avoid stacking medications. Drugs may not work until rewarmed.
